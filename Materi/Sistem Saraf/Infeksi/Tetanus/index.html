    Anamnesis

    1 Anamnesis:

    1. Incubation Period: The incubation period for tetanus can vary from 2 days to greater than 4 weeks, with the majority of cases occurring within 14 days. This is defined as the time from the initial injury to the first symptom (e.g., trismus).
    2. Initial Symptoms: Common symptoms at presentation include trismus or "lockjaw," dysphagia, neck, back, and shoulder pain, and stiffness. Isolated trismus is the most common subtle finding at the time of presentation.
    3. Progression of Symptoms: As the disease progresses, patients develop generalized muscle rigidity and spasms, with manifestations occurring spontaneously or being precipitated by external stimuli such as touch, light, or noise.
    4. Facial Symptoms: Sardonic smile (risus sardonicus) is a classical finding due to sustained contraction and rigidity of facial muscles.
    5. Opisthotonos: The presence of opisthotonos, where the head and feet are drawn backward, and the spine arches forward, is another classical finding.
    6. Autonomic Dysfunction: Elevated levels of catecholamines lead to a hypersympathetic state and autonomic dysfunction, typically occurring around the second week of the disease, with manifestations including fever, blood pressure extremes, and cardiac arrhythmias.1

    2 Anamnesis:

    1. Incubation Period: Ask about any recent injuries or wounds, as the incubation period for tetanus is around 7–14 days.
    2. Onset of Symptoms: Inquire about the time course from the first symptom to the first spasm, which is usually 2–5 days.2

    3 Anamnesis:

    1. Incubation Period: Inquire about the time from exposure to disease, as tetanus has an incubation period ranging from 3 days to 3 weeks.
    2. Onset of Symptoms: Ask about the gradual onset of symptoms over 1 day to 1 week, progressing to painful generalized muscle spasm.
    3. Severity Factors: Explore factors that might influence severity, such as heavily contaminated wounds leading to a more rapid onset.3

    4 Anamnesis

    • Incubation Period and Period of Onset: The time from the wound to the first symptom (incubation period) and the time from the first symptom to the first generalized spasm (period of onset) are crucial for assessing prognosis. Shorter times are associated with a worse outcome.
    • Neonatal Tetanus: In neonates, the younger the infant is when symptoms occur, the worse the prognosis. Presentation includes an inability to suck.4

    5 Anamnesis:

    1. Wound History: Tetanus can result from wounds contaminated with toxin-producing C. tetani. It is important to inquire about the type of wound (puncture, laceration, abrasion) and any recent surgical procedures, otitis media, abortion, or injection drug use.

    2. Incubation Period: Understanding the incubation period is crucial. Tetanus can have a short incubation period (<24 hours to >1 month), and short periods are associated with severe disease and a poor prognosis.

    3. Clinical Presentation: Patients with generalized tetanus often present with pain and stiffness in the masseter muscles ("lockjaw"). Asking about symptoms progression from facial muscles to neck, trunk, and extremities is relevant. Note the characteristic expressions like risus sardonicus (sardonic smile) and the development of trismus.

    4. Complications: Inquire about complications such as rhabdomyolysis and long-bone fractures secondary to violent muscle contractions. Note that mental status remains normal unless respiratory compromise occurs.

    5. Neonatal Tetanus: For infants, ask about immunization status of the mother, and if there's a history of unsterile treatment of the umbilical cord stump. Symptoms in neonatal tetanus include weakness, irritability, and an inability to suck.6

    6 Anamnesis (Medical History):

    1. Incubation Period: It is mentioned that tetanus has an incubation period ranging from 3 to 21 days. Inquiring about recent injuries or wounds within this timeframe may be relevant.

    2. Period of Onset: Understanding the time from the first symptom to the first generalized spasm is crucial, as shorter periods indicate a worse prognosis.

    3. Portals of Entry: Certain portals of entry, such as compound fractures, are associated with poorer prognoses. Inquiring about recent injuries, especially those involving potential contamination, is important.

    4. Patient Characteristics: Tetanus may be particularly severe in narcotics addicts. Inquiring about substance use history may be relevant.

    5. Symptoms: Understanding the symptoms, such as risus sardonicus, trismus, and abdominal rigidity, helps in identifying the onset and progression of the disease.

    6. Triggers for Spasms: The spasms in tetanus are often triggered by sensory stimuli. Inquiring about any specific triggers experienced by the patient may provide additional diagnostic information.

    7. Medical History of Immunization: Understanding the patient's immunization history is crucial, especially for assessing the risk of recurrent tetanus.7

    Pemeriksaan Fisik

    1 Physical Examination:

    1. Muscle Rigidity and Spasms: Generalized tetanus is characterized by muscle rigidity and spasms throughout the body, while local tetanus manifests with rigidity and spasms around the inciting wound.
    2. Painful Muscles: In local tetanus, affected muscles around the wound are painful at rest and during contractions.
    3. Deep Tendon Reflexes: In local tetanus, deep tendon reflexes are more pronounced.
    4. Cephalic Tetanus Specifics: For cephalic tetanus, where cranial nerves are affected, the initial presentation includes trismus and cranial nerve palsy, most commonly affecting the facial nerve.
    5. Neonatal Tetanus: For neonatal tetanus, it occurs in newborns of mothers who are unvaccinated or inadequately vaccinated. Clinical manifestations within 10 days of birth include generalized weakness, inability to suck, and irritability.1

    2 Physical Examination:

    1. Muscle Spasms: Check for any signs of skeletal muscle spasms, especially in the facial muscles (trismus or 'lockjaw' and risus sardonicus) and extensor spinae (opisthotonus).
    2. Facial Features: Look for the characteristic risus sardonicus, a facial expression caused by spasm of facial muscles.
    3. Respiratory Function: Assess respiratory function, as severe spasms can impede respiration. Note any signs of increased respiratory muscle tone, respiratory distress, or changes in breathing pattern.
    4. Blood Pressure and Heart Rate: Monitor for signs of dysfunction in the autonomic nervous system, such as fluctuating blood pressure and variable heart rate.
    5. Neonatal Presentation: For neonatal tetanus, inquire about difficulty feeding followed by frank spasms in infants. Note the World Health Organization (WHO) case definition for neonatal tetanus.2

    3 Physical Examination

    1. Generalized Tetanus Symptoms:
    2. Lockjaw (trismus)
    3. Risus sardonicus (a characteristic facial expression with raised eyebrows and a fixed smile)

    4. Severe muscle spasms involving paraspinal muscles

    5. Cephalic Tetanus:

    6. Impairment of cranial nerves

    7. Associated with infected wounds around the neck and head

    8. Localized Tetanus:

    9. Localized pain progressing to weakness around the wound

    10. Progression to localized muscle spasms or rigidity

    11. Neonatal Tetanus:

    12. Occurs in newborn infants due to the absence of protective maternal immunity

    13. Results from infection of the umbilical stump after delivery outside the healthcare environment or from cultural practices involving the application of dirt or cow dung to the umbilical stump

    14. General Symptoms:

    15. Difficulty swallowing
    16. Fever and sweating
    17. Headache
    18. Labile blood pressure and tachycardia
    19. Painful muscle stiffness of the body
    20. Sudden involuntary muscle tightening or spasms3

    4 Physical Examination:

    • Initial Symptoms: Common initial symptoms include trismus (lockjaw), muscle pain and stiffness, back pain, and difficulty swallowing.
    • Progression of Symptoms: As the disease progresses, generalized muscle spasm develops. The involvement of laryngeal muscles early in the course can lead to life-threatening airway obstruction.
    • Respiratory Involvement: Respiratory muscle spasms result in respiratory failure. Ventilatory support is crucial to prevent death, as respiratory failure is a common cause of death in tetanus.
    • Autonomic Disturbance: Autonomic involvement is maximal during the second week of severe tetanus. This is evidenced by labile blood pressure, rapid fluctuations from high to low accompanied by tachycardia, episodes of bradycardia, and heart block.
    • Other Autonomic Symptoms: Autonomic involvement is also evidenced by gastrointestinal stasis, sweating, increased tracheal secretions, and acute (often high-output) renal failure.4

    5 Physical Examination:

    1. Muscle Rigidity and Contractions: Assess for generalized muscular rigidity, reflex convulsive spasms, and tonic muscle contractions. Note the progression from muscle stiffness to rigidity.

    2. Facial and Cranial Nerve Involvement: Examine for involvement of facial muscles, cranial nerves (especially in cephalic tetanus), and signs like risus sardonicus.

    3. Autonomic Dysfunction: During the second week of clinical generalized tetanus, look for signs of autonomic dysfunction such as tachycardia, labile hypertension, profuse sweating, hyperpyrexia, and increased urinary excretion of catecholamines.

    4. Respiratory Symptoms: Monitor for laryngospasm or contraction of respiratory muscles that could result in respiratory compromise. Aspiration pneumonia is common in autopsied cases.6

    6 Physical Examination:

    1. Initial Signs: Generalized tetanus often begins with risus sardonicus and trismus. Look for signs of increased tone in the orbicularis oris and masseter rigidity.

    2. Abdominal Rigidity: Abdominal rigidity may be present in generalized tetanus. Palpation and examination of the abdomen may reveal this symptom.

    3. Spasm Characteristics: The generalized spasm in tetanus resembles decorticate posturing with flexion of the arms and extension of the legs. Observing and documenting the characteristics of spasms is essential.

    4. Autonomic Dysfunction: Symptoms of autonomic hyperactivity, such as hypertension, tachycardia, and hyperthermia, may be present. Monitoring vital signs is crucial.

    5. Neonatal Tetanus: For neonatal tetanus, inquire about the infection of the umbilical stump and any cultural practices that might contribute. Assessing generalized weakness, failure to nurse, and later rigidity and spasms is essential.7
